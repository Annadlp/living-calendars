Naukan Yupik · Nuvukahmit · Chukchi Peninsula, Arctic Russia

Living Calendars,
Shifting Seasons

The Naukan Yupik people of Chukotka developed a calendar structured entirely around what the land, sea, and animals do. This platform documents that knowledge — and records how the Arctic is changing, witnessed by those who have watched it longest.

"The grandfather always told the story of how he caught the bearded seal. He never brought a ringed seal into the yaranga without giving it water to drink first." — From Yaakov Tagyek's account. Ecological knowledge encoded in ritual.

The calendar

About this calendar

A year of watching

The Naukan were forcibly displaced from their ancestral village on the Chukchi Peninsula in 1958 by Soviet authorities. But the knowledge survived — in elders' memories, in community records, in oral tradition passed through families. What you see here is one of the few surviving documents of its kind.

Each month name in the Naukan language is itself an observation. The calendar was structured not around dates, but around what the world does: when seal pups appear on the ice, when the skin boat is lowered, when the drum is beaten.

This record was compiled from community oral history, including the account of Yaakov Tagyek, whose family belonged to the Imtugmit clan.

The Naukan Yupik seasonal calendar

Twelve months of ecological observation

Documented from community records and oral tradition. Each month name encodes an observation about the natural world.

01 · January

Qatngoq

The beginning.

Seasonal practice Праздник кита Пол'а — the whale festival, lasting until mid-January. Hunting of ringed seal. Those who had killed a bowhead whale were not permitted to hunt during this period: the prohibition was law.

02 · February

Kanghuyaseq

Hoarfrost appears.

Seasonal practice Hunting of bearded seal (лахтак) and ringed seal (нерпа). A hunter who killed two ringed seals in one outing received a name for it: асымъи. Skill and provision were named and remembered.

03 · March

Nayeeghakhsiq

Seal pups appear on the ice.

Seasonal practice Hunting continues. The ribbon seal (к'айг'улъык) arrives along the coast; hunting of ribbon seals begins. Ice conditions must be read with care. The pups are a clock: when they appear, the season has turned.

04 · April

Kypngyghahsiq

Days grow longer. The skin boat is lowered.

Seasonal practice К'амиг'ал'ак' — the ceremony of launching the umiak. The beginning of walrus hunting. Active walrus migration along the Chukchi coast. The major open-water season begins.

05 · May

Kuighyt Anit

The mother of rivers.

Seasonal observation Active walrus migration continues. Rivers begin to open and pour into the sea. Ice breaks from coastal waterways. Subsistence hunting at sea intensifies. The land is moving.

06 · June

Nunam Qumaghve

The land dries out.

Seasonal observation The tundra thaws and dries after winter. Coastal terrain becomes accessible on foot. Plant gathering begins. The shift from sea to land — the year's balance point.

07 · July

Nunivivik

The month of gathering plants.

Seasonal practice The dedicated season for gathering tundra plants — roots, greens, coastal vegetation. Foods gathered and preserved for winter stores. A month of abundance after the long cold.

08 · August

—

When the leaves harden.

Seasonal observation The shift in leaf texture signals late summer. Berry season. Hunters begin reading signs of the approaching freeze — watching ice, wind patterns, and animal behavior for what is coming.

09 · September

Amighayaq

The molt.

Seasonal observation The molting season for marine mammals. A transitional month — sea and animals changing, the hunter's year turning. The community shifts toward winter preparation, watching water and sky.

10 · October

Purughneghvik

Thick wet snow. Snow haze.

Seasonal observation The arrival of пуруга — the dense, wet coastal snowstorm characteristic of Chukotka. Visibility drops. First sea ice forms. Hunters must know when to stay on shore. The year closes inward.

11 · November

Sauyaghvik

Time to beat the drum.

Seasonal practice The season of celebration, drumming, and communal gathering as deep winter closes in. Knowledge passes between generations — stories, songs, oral history. The clan of Yaakov Tagyek's grandmother, the Маюгегмит, were known as singers.

12 · December

Aqumtuq

The sun sets.

Seasonal observation The polar night. The sun drops below the horizon and the community turns inward. Trap lines for arctic fox (песец) — the fox was struck on the head, so as not to damage the fur. Hide preparation, bone carving, waiting.

Naukan · Наукан

A village that no longer exists

The Naukan Yupik lived on the Chukchi Peninsula for centuries — on a steep coast facing Alaska, twelve kilometres from Cape Dezhnev. In 1958, Soviet authorities forcibly relocated the entire community. The village was abandoned. The calendar remained.

The platform

Two layers. One living record.

The platform does two things simultaneously — and the meaning comes from holding them together.

Layer 1 — Traditional knowledge

Working with elders and cultural institutions, we document the structure of traditional seasonal calendars: what events were expected each month, what species were observed, what environmental signals marked time — and the observational logic behind them. The Naukan calendar is the first case study and proof of concept.

Layer 2 — What is happening now

Community members record what they observe today. When animals arrived earlier or later than remembered. When ice became safe — and when it stopped being safe at the expected time. When seasonal rituals could no longer be performed. The drift between the calendar and reality is the climate signal.

Each participating community controls its own data — what is shared publicly, what remains internal, and how contributions are attributed. Indigenous data sovereignty is a design principle, not an afterthought.

About this project

This platform was created by Aivana Enmynkau — granddaughter of Лъугагун, a Naukan sea mammal hunter of Chukotka.

The Naukan calendar at the heart of this project is not an abstraction. It is the living record of a family, a community, and a coast — passed down through generations of hunters, walrus riders, and drummers. The knowledge encoded in each month name was earned across centuries of watching.

Stories from the coast

Fiction

These stories were written by Aivana Enmynkau. They take place on the same coast as the calendar — in the same tundra, the same winter light. They are not documentation. They are what documentation cannot hold.

I

Anuka's Gambit

I have played chess for forty years, and in forty years I have seen everything.

I have seen a teenager in Tbilisi checkmate in three moves a man who had been teaching him since morning. I have seen a blind old man in Kharkov who knew eight hundred games by heart and could reproduce any of them. I have seen people weep after a loss — not from hurt, but from the beauty of the game.

But I had never seen anything like this.

His name was Anuka. I don't know whether it was a name or a nickname — I never asked, and then there was no one left to ask. He lived in a coastal village on Chukotka, hunted sea mammals, and when I came to search his home in October of thirty-seven, he was perhaps sixty years old. A dark, weathered face. Hands so large it was hard to imagine them holding anything smaller than a harpoon.

On the table stood a chessboard.

Not homemade, not local bone carvings — a real set, European, darkened with age, with one missing pawn replaced by a smooth river stone. I stopped. My men continued the search — overturning bundles, checking the floors — while I stood and looked at the board.

"Yours?" I asked.

He sat against the wall and looked at me without the expression I was used to seeing during searches. No fear, no anger, no pleading in his eyes. He simply looked.

"Mine," he said.

"Do you play?"

A pause. Then: "I play."

They found the money twenty minutes later. American dollars, wrapped in leather, in a recess under the floor. Not much — by the standards of serious cases, an entirely insignificant sum. But currency is currency, there had been a denunciation, the case was already moving in its own direction, and we both knew it.

I could have left immediately. Protocol, signature, transport to Petropavlovsk.

I sat down at the table. "Shall we play?" I said.

He looked at me for a long moment. Not with hope — I want to emphasize this, because it matters. Not with hope that a game might change anything. Simply as though he wanted to know me better. Then he sat down opposite and arranged the pieces.

I had been playing since I was fourteen. My father taught me, his father taught him, and somewhere in that chain there was a man who had learned from a real master. I knew all the classical openings, several hundred analyzed games, endgame theory. In Leningrad I played at a club and won more often than I lost.

The first five moves were ordinary. I played e4, he responded in standard fashion, I developed my pieces — all familiar, all within what I had seen before.

On the sixth move he sacrificed a pawn. I took it. Of course I took it — the sacrifice looked like an error or a weakness, and I took automatically, the way one takes the obvious.

On the seventh he sacrificed a knight.

I stopped. Looked at the board. Looked at him. He sat with the same face — no expression whatsoever. Hands on his knees. Waiting.

I took the knight.

For the next ten moves I tried to understand what was happening. He was not attacking — not in the sense in which people attack. He was applying pressure from everywhere at once, from sacrifices, from apparent weaknesses, from positions I did not know how to read because I had never seen anything like them. It resembled none of the styles I knew. Not Steinitz, not Chigorin, not any of the modern players. It was something else. Something his own.

I was losing gradually, the way one loses ground in the dark — not all at once, centimeter by centimeter, and each time it seems one can still recover, and each time one cannot.

The checkmate was beautiful. I mean objectively beautiful, in the sense that word applies to chess: inevitable and yet unexpected, simple in the end and incomprehensible along the way.

I stared at the board for a long time afterward. Then I looked up. He was waiting. As before — hands on his knees, face composed.

I extended my hand. He shook it — firmly, without theater.

"Where did you learn this?" I asked.

"On a whaling ship," he said. "American. I was young." "Who taught you?" "The boatswain. An old man. He died there, on the ship. He left me the board and the money."

The same money that was now entered in the search record.

I stood. I told him he had until morning. That transport would come at seven. That this was all I could do.

He nodded. Without gratitude — simply nodded, the way people nod who have understood everything before it was said.

I left.

In the morning they found him. I will not describe it — that is not the part of the story worth telling in detail.

The chess set remained on the table. Arranged — not in the starting position, but in the final one, where checkmate stood. He had set it up again after I left. Why — I don't know. Perhaps he simply wanted to look at it one more time.

I took the board. I had no right to, strictly speaking, but I took it. No one objected — who would object, and about what.

That evening I reconstructed the game — wrote it down from memory, move by move. I looked for logic, looked for a system, tried to understand where it came from — what tradition, what school. Nowhere. He had invented it himself. On the ship, in his free time, with a boatswain who was probably just a decent player — nothing more. And from that, from nothing, from tundra and sea and long winters, he had grown something that existed in no textbook.

I showed the game to several people — carefully, without names, simply as a study. A master from Moscow looked at it for twenty minutes, then said: this is wrong, people don't play like this, these are mistakes. I asked: then why did white lose? He didn't answer.

Another — an old man from Tbilisi — looked at it and said: this is a gambit. Peculiar, strange, but a gambit. What is it called?

I thought for a moment. "Anuka's Gambit," I said.

He knew from the first move how the evening would end. Not the game — the evening. He sat across from me, and he knew everything, and he played anyway. Not to win anything beyond the game. Simply because it was his game — his — and he wanted to play it one more time, properly, completely, before someone who could appreciate it.

He won the game. He lost everything else. And yet — I have thought about this for a long time, and I think about it still — it is not clear which of us rose from the table that morning with more.

The board stands on my shelf. The position unchanged — as he left it. The final one.

Invisible Villages

Letters from the North

Dear Andrei Petrovich,

You asked me to write about my practice. I am writing about villages. Not the ones that appear on maps. The ones that actually exist.

I. The Village That Only Women Remember

It was called Orvynayvaam. I wrote the name in my notebook because I was afraid of forgetting it — it is made of sounds I have no habit for.

The men left at different times and for different reasons. Some for work, some for vodka, some simply walked into the tundra one day and did not return — not necessarily because they died, they simply did not return. The records burned in seventy-four along with the administrative office. According to official data, Orvynayvaam was resettled in sixty-eight.

Four women call themselves residents of Orvynayvaam.

They live in Anadyr, in concrete apartment blocks, in different stairwells. They meet on Fridays at the oldest woman's home. I attended one such meeting — a paramedic I worked with brought me along. They drank tea and spoke in a language I don't understand. Occasionally one of them would say a word and they would laugh — not the way people laugh at a joke, but the way they laugh when something has been named precisely.

I asked the paramedic: what are they talking about?

She listened and said: about how the shore looks there, from a particular spot, at a particular time of year. They cannot agree on which direction to look in order to see it correctly.

Orvynayvaam exists in four versions simultaneously. When the last of them dies, none will remain. Or one will. A shared one. Which was never the truth of any one of them individually, but will be the truth of all four together.

I don't know what to make of this from a medical standpoint. I am writing to you.

Nina Enmynkau. Stoibishche moyego detstva (The Campsite of My Childhood)

Nina Sergeyevna Enmynkau was born in 1935 in the Naukan village on the Chukchi Peninsula, in the family of the hereditary sea hunter Lъuaun. She became one of the first Yupik radio journalists, broadcasting in indigenous languages across Chukotka and Alaska — documenting the lives of walrus hunters, reindeer herders, and coastal communities before those worlds were irrevocably changed.

This collection — published in 2024 — gathers her radio broadcasts, interviews, and field reports. It is an irreplaceable archive of a world that no longer exists in the form she recorded it. She is among the last living speakers of the Nuvukahmit language.

Published by Almaz-Graf, St. Petersburg, 2024 · Inuit Circumpolar Council Chukotka
